Kanchipuram: Where History Adorns Every Stone


Kanchipuram, often hailed as the "City of a Thousand Temples," is a sacred pilgrimage site nestled in the heart of Tamil Nadu, India. Renowned for its rich heritage and architectural marvels, this ancient city boasts a tapestry of temples dating back to several centuries. Each temple in Kanchipuram stands as a testament to the architectural brilliance and spiritual fervor of its builders.


These temples not only serve as centers of religious worship but also as living repositories of art, culture, and spirituality. They attract devotees, historians, and tourists alike, offering a glimpse into the rich cultural heritage and ancient traditions of South India. Beyond their religious significance, the temples of Kanchipuram are cherished for their serene ambiance, intricate craftsmanship, and timeless beauty, making them an integral part of India's architectural and spiritual legacy.

Kanchi Kamakshi Amman


The sanctum sanctorum of the Kanchi Kamakshi Amman Temple houses the presiding deity, Goddess Kamakshi, The image of the main deity is seated in a Padmasana, a yogic posture signifying peace and prosperity, instead of the traditional standing pose. The goddess holds a sugarcane bow and bunch of five flowers in the lower two of her arms and has a pasha (lasso), an ankusha (goad) in her upper two arms. A parrot perches near the flower bunch. There are no other goddess temples in the city of Kanchipuram, apart from this one, which is unusual in a city that has hundreds of traditional temples. There are various legends that account for this fact.


This is the only temple in India where goddess Tripura Sundari is depicted the same way she is depicted in Lalitha Sahasranamam. This temple is also mentioned in the Lalitha Sahasranamam.

History


It is believed that Goddess Kamakshi came out of this Bilaakasha to slay demons. The devas had worshipped Adi Para shakti here at bilakasam or kamakoti in the form of parrots, to free them from the clutches of the demon Bhandakasura, after goddess tripura Sundari slayed the demon Bhandakasura along with his subbordinates in the form of Bala.

Architecture


The Kamakshi Amman Temple in Kanchipuram showcases exquisite South Indian architecture with its towering entrance gopuram, intricately carved vimana, and ornate sculptures depicting various deities and mythical beings. Surrounding the main sanctum are smaller shrines dedicated to other gods and goddesses, all adorned with elaborate carvings and frescoes. The temple's thousand-pillared hall, "Aayiram Kaal Mandapam," is a marvel of craftsmanship, featuring intricately carved pillars showcasing divine motifs. Overall, the temple's architecture is a captivating blend of artistic splendor and spiritual reverence.


This temple houses shrines for Ganapati, Subramanya, Adi Shankaracharya, Dharma Sastha, Raja Shyamala, Varahi and many more deities.

Religious Significance


The Kamakshi Amman Temple is considered one of the 18 Maha Shakti Peethas, sacred sites where parts of the body of Goddess Sati (an incarnation of Goddess Parvati) fell after her body was dismembered by Lord Vishnu's Sudarshana Chakra. It is believed that the Goddess Kamakshi's navel (Nabhisthana) is associated with this temple.


The temple has associations with Adi Shankaracharya, the great philosopher-saint who propagated Advaita Vedanta. It is believed that Adi Shankaracharya installed the Sri Chakra, a sacred geometric diagram representing the goddess's divine energy, in the temple.


This temple also houses one of the 108 divya desam dedicated to Sri Vishnu as Kalvar Perumal.
